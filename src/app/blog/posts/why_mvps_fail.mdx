# Good design is good business
## A series about the untapped business impact of good design. Part 1: why MVPs are usually a waste of resources.

### The shotgun approach: cram as many features as you can
Most ventures, projects or organisations pride themselves on applying the principle of agile product delivery, as laid out in The Lean Startup, by Eric Ries. One of the pillars of this approach consists in building a product fast and releasing it to the market as fast as you can. The goal: test your initial hypotheses, before you commit too many resources to your v1. It’s a great idea in theory but unfortunately, it often fails on implementation.
In my years as designer and builder, I have rarely seen this concept properly used. Most projects and ventures I’ve worked with have impatient stakeholders and very limited resources. 
They usually dedicate most of them to cramming as many features as they can into their “MVP”, thinking that “more features = more value”. As such, MVPs are just another milestone on a rigid roadmap, living little wriggle room for experimentation… or error. 

### No room for error - when you actually need mistakes to learn
Although reassuring for the venture leads, this “shotgun approach” leaves little room for thinking about WHAT to build. 
You spend so much time building that there is no time to think of what the market expects, what KPIs define success and what actually brings value to the user. 
In engineering-heavy cultures, such as Web3, it’s almost a point of pride to build without thinking, as if a product well built was meant to find its market. It’s magical thinking, but it is tempting - if you are ok to avoid the questions that are existential to your venture.
I’ve seen countless projects hit the wall for this exact reason. No audience, no revenue and no cash left to pivot. Resources have been squandered into an MVP before the founders did any reasonable market research. 

### MVPs are tools - not the product itself
I think we often misunderstand what MVPs are for. Fundamentally, they are tools to help you decide WHAT to build, not how or how fast. It falls under the same category of tools as market research, archetypes, personas or business canvases. These tools help you figure out what you product should be and who it is for. 

An MVP is a tool of exploration, and as such, we have to be ok with letting them go to waste. Nonetheless, because resources are scarce, and because most of it is dedicated to building fast, projects usually end up with a bloated MVP, no cash in the bank, facing the impossibility of admitting failure, even if the learnings were invaluable. 

Tough luck, but there is hope. You dont have to fall into this trap and if you devote just a little bit of time to think about what you want to build, you’ll reap immense benefits, far more significant than developing the next feature no one needs. The Minimal Viable Product is an interesting tool in your arsenal, but it is an expensive one. In part 2, we’ll see how you can explore your market using affordable but efficient tools that any senior designer should have in his belt. 

Have a good one!
